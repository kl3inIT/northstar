# IELTS-style Speaking practice rubric

Provenance: criterion names and scoring intent follow the public IELTS Speaking
band descriptors published by the IELTS partners. The summaries below are
Northstar's compact paraphrases for an unofficial practice estimator, not a
replacement for the official descriptors:

- https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf
- https://ielts.org/take-a-test/your-results/ielts-scoring-in-detail

The official assessment has four equally weighted criteria. This one-answer
practice estimate cannot reproduce a full Part 1-3 examiner interaction, so use
ranges and conservative confidence.

## FC — Fluency and Coherence

- Band 9: sustained, fully coherent speech with hesitation used for content,
  not language search; cohesion is consistently natural.
- Band 8: fluent and well developed with only occasional language-related
  hesitation or repetition; ideas remain coherent and relevant.
- Band 7: can speak at length with some hesitation, repetition, or
  self-correction; discourse markers and idea development are generally sound.
- Band 6: willing to continue at length but coherence sometimes weakens through
  hesitation, repetition, or mechanical linking.
- Band 5: flow is usually maintainable but relies on repetition,
  self-correction, slow speech, or overused connectors; complex ideas are hard
  to sustain.
- Band 4: frequent pauses and repetition cause noticeable breakdowns; answers
  are short or loosely connected.
- Band 3 or below: long pauses and fragmentary responses prevent sustained,
  coherent communication.

For one answer, combine transcript organization with measured duration, speech
rate, provider fluency, and visible repetition. Do not treat every natural
spoken filler as an error.

## LR — Lexical Resource

- Band 9: vocabulary is precise, flexible, natural, and idiomatic across the
  topic, with only rare slips.
- Band 8: wide and flexible vocabulary conveys precise meaning; uncommon or
  idiomatic choices are mostly skilful despite occasional inaccuracies.
- Band 7: sufficient flexibility for varied topics, with some less-common or
  idiomatic language and effective paraphrase despite occasional errors.
- Band 6: enough vocabulary to discuss the topic at length; meaning is clear
  despite inappropriate choices, and paraphrase is generally possible.
- Band 5: adequate for familiar ideas but limited flexibility and paraphrase;
  noticeable word-choice errors remain.
- Band 4: basic vocabulary handles familiar meaning but repetition and errors
  restrict unfamiliar ideas.
- Band 3 or below: very limited vocabulary permits only simple personal
  information or isolated meaning.

Judge the words actually present in the transcript. Do not reward long words by
themselves and do not invent collocation errors without quoting them.

## GRA — Grammatical Range and Accuracy

- Band 9: structures are varied, flexible, and almost always accurate, with
  only rare slips.
- Band 8: a wide range of structures is used flexibly; most sentences are
  error-free and errors are occasional.
- Band 7: varied complex structures are attempted; many sentences are
  error-free and remaining errors do not reduce intelligibility.
- Band 6: a mix of simple and complex forms is used, but complex structures
  contain frequent errors; communication usually remains clear.
- Band 5: basic forms are reasonably controlled while complex attempts are
  limited and error-prone, sometimes forcing reformulation.
- Band 4: basic sentence forms dominate and subordinate structures are rare;
  frequent errors can impede meaning.
- Band 3 or below: sentence structure is severely limited and errors dominate
  beyond memorized or very simple forms.

Spoken contractions and informal register are valid. Quote exact transcript
evidence and distinguish systematic errors from transcription punctuation.

## P — Pronunciation

- Band 9: a full range of pronunciation features is precise and effortless to
  understand; accent never affects intelligibility.
- Band 8: a wide range of features is sustained with only occasional lapses;
  speech is easy to understand throughout.
- Band 7: feature control is generally effective with some lapses; speech is
  easy to understand despite some accent influence.
- Band 6: feature control is mixed but generally intelligible; individual words
  or sounds sometimes reduce clarity.
- Band 5: some effective features are present, but inconsistency and
  mispronounced sounds sometimes require listener effort.
- Band 4: a limited feature range and frequent sound problems cause regular
  intelligibility difficulty.
- Band 3 or below: pronunciation problems make much of the response difficult
  to understand.

The text grader did not hear the recording. Ground this criterion only in the
provider's pronunciation, prosody, fluency, and word/phoneme accuracy evidence.
Never infer accent, pitch, stress, or listener effort when those facts are not
present in the evidence envelope.
